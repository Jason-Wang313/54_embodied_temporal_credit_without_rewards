\begin{abstract}
Temporal credit assignment in robot learning is usually routed through scalar rewards, shaped progress terms, hindsight relabels, or delayed task labels. Embodied systems contain another signal: physical effect traces. Contact onset, object displacement, force impulses, support-graph edits, and constraint releases can identify which earlier action changed the world before reward is available. The first version of this paper made that claim too broadly. A v2 hardening pass showed that raw effect traces win when events are reliable or partially missed, but lose to dense shaping when events are misbound or adversarial. We therefore introduce \method, an event-audited trace-credit protocol that uses physical traces only when event coverage, false-event rate, and action-event binding are sufficiently reliable, and otherwise falls back or abstains. A deterministic full-scale benchmark spans 12 task families, 6 event channels, 6 temporal horizons, 6 event-reliability regimes, 5 proxy/reward regimes, 5 sensor/binding regimes, and 8 credit protocols, yielding 518,400 compact condition rows representing 176,504,832,000 evaluations and 16,944,463,872,000 timestep decisions. \method is the best non-oracle method with utility 0.758, localization error 2.772, precision 0.609, reward dependence 0.014, and miscredit risk 0.235. The oracle remains best overall with utility 1.000. \rawtrace remains an essential negative control: it fails under misbinding and adversarial events. The contribution is a benchmark and reporting discipline for reward-free embodied temporal credit, not a real-robot deployment proof.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Robot learning often treats reward as the carrier of blame and credit. A reward arrives late, a temporal-difference update propagates it backward, and a policy eventually discovers which earlier action mattered. This framing is powerful, but it is oddly disembodied. A robot can see an object move, feel a contact transition, observe a force impulse, or detect that a constraint has released. Those events are not rewards. They are physical evidence that an earlier action changed the world.

The first version of this project argued that such effect traces can assign temporal credit before scalar reward. The mechanism was plausible and the first diagnostic looked strong: effect-trace credit reduced localization error from 3.550 for delayed-reward TD and 2.342 for dense shaping to 0.862. That was not enough. The old diagnostic assumed usable events.

Submission hardening added a v2 event-reliability stress. Under reliable events, effect-trace error was 1.014 versus dense shaping 2.349. Under missed events, effect-trace error was 2.243 versus dense shaping 2.405. But under misbound events, effect-trace error rose to 2.679 while dense shaping was 2.438. Under adversarial events, effect-trace error was 4.222 while dense shaping was 2.375. The old positive claim was therefore too broad.

This paper rebuilds the claim around auditing. The question is no longer:
\begin{quote}
Can physical effect traces carry temporal credit?
\end{quote}
The stronger question is:
\begin{quote}
When can a robot trust physical effect traces, and when should credit assignment fall back, abstain, or admit that dense shaping is safer?
\end{quote}

We answer with \method, an event-audited trace-credit protocol and benchmark. The protocol estimates event coverage, action-event binding, false-event rate, and proxy conflict. It uses traces when they are reliable, downweights or falls back when coverage is weak, and avoids false confidence when events are misbound or adversarial.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
First, we preserve the v2 failure of raw effect traces as a negative control. Second, we introduce a full-scale deterministic benchmark across task, event, horizon, reliability, proxy, and sensor/binding axes. Third, we evaluate delayed-reward TD, dense shaping, hindsight relabel credit, inverse-dynamics credit, raw effect trace, coverage-weighted trace, event-audited trace, and oracle causal event credit. Fourth, we show that \method is the best non-oracle method while oracle remains best and raw traces still fail under bad event binding. Fifth, we provide generated tables, figures, validation metadata, and a reporting checklist.

\section{Related Work And Novelty Boundary}

Temporal credit assignment is central to reinforcement learning. Temporal-difference learning propagates delayed scalar reward. Eligibility traces smear credit backward over recent states and actions. Hindsight methods relabel failed attempts as successes under alternate goals. Dense shaping injects proxy progress terms. Self-supervised robot learning trains predictive models, inverse models, or latent dynamics from interaction logs.

This paper does not claim that reward-free learning, hindsight relabeling, causal inference, tactile event detection, or robot world models are new. It also does not claim that physical events are sufficient for policy learning. The novelty boundary is narrower: the paper treats physical effect events as temporal-credit supervision, then audits when that supervision is reliable enough to use.

The v2 failure makes the boundary sharper. A raw trace can be worse than dense shaping if the event is assigned to the wrong action. A support-graph edit, force impulse, or object motion event is useful only if the robot knows whether the event covered the true effect, whether it was false, and whether it was bound to the correct action window. The final claim is therefore about event-audited trace credit, not trace credit as a universal replacement for rewards.

\section{Problem Setup}

Consider a trajectory $(\states_1,\actions_1,\ldots,\states_T)$ with no immediate scalar reward. Let $e_t$ be a detected physical effect event. The event might be a new contact, an object displacement, a force impulse, a constraint release, or a support-graph edit. A temporal credit method assigns responsibility to earlier actions.

A delayed-reward learner waits for a terminal scalar reward $R_T$. A dense shaping learner uses a proxy progress signal $\phi(\states_t)$. A trace method uses local embodied events:
\begin{equation}
    c_{i,t} =
    p(e_t \mid \actions_i,\states_i,\states_{i+1:t-1})
    -
    p(e_t \mid \mathrm{do}(\actions_i=\varnothing),\states_i,\states_{i+1:t-1}).
\end{equation}
This expression is a conceptual target, not a required estimator. The practical problem is that $e_t$ may be missing, false, stale, or bound to the wrong action. Therefore the estimator must audit the trace before treating it as credit.

\subsection{Event Coverage}

Coverage is the probability that the event detector observes the physical effect that matters. Low coverage is not always fatal: a high-precision event stream can still be useful if the method knows when it is missing information. Hidden low coverage is fatal because the method may assign no credit or assign credit only to visible effects.

\subsection{Action-Event Binding}

Binding is the assignment between an effect event and the action window that caused it. Binding errors are more dangerous than missed events. A missed event removes information; a misbound event points learning in the wrong direction. V2 showed that misbinding is the key failure mode for raw trace credit.

\subsection{False Events}

False events arise from distractor object motion, actuator latency, sensor spikes, or decoy contacts. False events can make a trace method confidently learn the wrong causal story. A method must estimate false-event rate or report that it cannot.

\subsection{Proxy Conflict}

Dense shaping can help when the proxy aligns with the true effect. It can hurt when the proxy points at a distractor. The benchmark therefore includes aligned dense shaping, deceptive dense shaping, no reward, sparse delayed reward, and conflicting human success labels.

\section{Credit Protocols}

\paragraph{Delayed-reward TD.}
This baseline waits for scalar reward and propagates it backward. It is robust to event-detector failure because it does not use event traces, but it localizes credit poorly over long horizons and depends completely on reward access.

\paragraph{Dense shaping proxy.}
This baseline assigns credit through a hand-shaped progress term. It is strong when the proxy is aligned and event traces are unreliable. It is weak when proxy progress is deceptive or reward labels conflict with the physical cause.

\paragraph{Hindsight relabel credit.}
This baseline relabels outcomes after the episode. It can recover useful supervision when goal labels are reliable, but it remains vulnerable to delayed decoys and conflicting labels.

\paragraph{Inverse-dynamics credit.}
This baseline credits actions by local predictability of state transitions. It is reward-free, but it tends to focus on immediate effects and can miss delayed causal responsibility.

\paragraph{\rawtrace.}
This is the original trace-credit mechanism. It uses detected events directly and does not audit coverage, false events, or binding. It should be strong under reliable events and weak under misbound or adversarial events.

\paragraph{Coverage-weighted trace.}
This ablation handles missed events by weighting trace credit by estimated coverage. It should improve over raw trace when coverage is low, but it should not solve misbinding because a covered event can still be assigned to the wrong action.

\paragraph{\method.}
\method estimates an audit score from coverage, binding, false-event rate, and adversarial risk. It uses effect traces when the score is high, falls back toward safer proxy credit when the score is intermediate, and abstains when the trace is untrustworthy.

\paragraph{Oracle causal event credit.}
The oracle observes the true causal event assignment. It is not deployable. It is an upper bound and a sanity check.

\section{Event-Audited Trace Credit}

\method computes an audit score
\begin{equation}
    a_t = g(\hat{\kappa}_t,\hat{\beta}_t,\hat{\eta}_t,\hat{\rho}_t),
\end{equation}
where $\hat{\kappa}_t$ is coverage, $\hat{\beta}_t$ is binding accuracy, $\hat{\eta}_t$ is false-event rate, and $\hat{\rho}_t$ is adversarial or decoy risk. A high audit score means the trace is likely to carry valid credit. A low score means the trace may be missing, false, or misbound.

The final credit signal is
\begin{equation}
    \hat{c}_{i,t} =
    (1-f_t-z_t)c^{trace}_{i,t}
    + f_t c^{fallback}_{i,t},
\end{equation}
where $f_t$ is fallback rate and $z_t$ is abstention. Fallback is not treated as free. The benchmark reports fallback, abstention, reward dependence, proxy regret, and miscredit risk so that the method cannot silently avoid hard cases.

This design directly answers the v2 stress. Reliable events should lead to trace use. Missed but precise events should lead to cautious use. Misbound and adversarial events should trigger fallback or abstention. The method should not pretend that physical traces are always trustworthy.

\section{Limitations}

The benchmark is deterministic and simulated. It does not contain real robot logs. It does not prove hardware safety. It does not solve event detection, multimodal synchronization, causal discovery, or policy learning end to end. The utility function penalizes miscredit and reward dependence strongly; other applications may choose different weights.

The final result also depends on having an event audit. If a robot cannot estimate coverage, false-event rate, or binding quality, it should not use the full \method claim. It may still use raw traces as a diagnostic, but it should report the v2 failure modes.

\section{Conclusion}

Embodied robots do not need to wait for scalar reward to know that an action changed the world. Physical effect traces can carry temporal credit, but only when their reliability is audited. The final Paper54 contribution is therefore not raw trace credit. It is event-audited trace credit: a benchmark, protocol, and reporting discipline that preserves the failure of misbound events while showing that audited traces are the best non-oracle credit signal in a large deterministic stress suite.

\appendix
\renewcommand{\thesection}{\arabic{section}}

\section{Task Families}

The benchmark includes block push with delayed displacement, door latch release, drawer opening, peg insertion with delayed seating, cable routing, cloth unfolding, tool use with delayed object motion, bimanual handoff, stacking with support creation, button press with delayed mechanism response, pouring with delayed flow onset, and recovery after failed contact.

These tasks were chosen because they stress different forms of temporal responsibility. Some have a clean mechanism event, such as a latch release. Some have ambiguous delayed effects, such as cloth unfolding or pouring. Some contain distractors, such as tool use with delayed object motion. Some make the causal action hard to identify because recovery actions occur after a failed contact.

\section{Event Channels}

The event channels are visual object displacement, contact onset or loss, force impulse, constraint release, support-graph edit, and multimodal fused event. Each channel has different coverage, binding, and false-event behavior. Visual displacement can be delayed and confounded by distractor objects. Contact onset is local and useful but can be ambiguous. Force impulses are sensitive and noisy. Constraint release is semantically clean but sparse. Support-graph edits are useful for stacking and handover. Multimodal fusion has higher coverage but inherits synchronization risk.

\section{Temporal Horizon Regimes}

The horizon regimes range from immediate 1--2 step effects to delayed decoys before the true event. Short horizons are easier for inverse dynamics. Medium and long horizons stress delayed reward and dense shaping. Variable multi-event horizons test whether a method can handle several effects in one episode. Delayed decoy horizons are the hardest because the first visible effect may not be the true causal target.

\section{Event Reliability Regimes}

Reliable events are the favorable case. Partially missed events test whether a method can use sparse high-quality evidence without hallucinating missing effects. High false-event rate tests sensitivity to distractors and sensor spikes. Action-event misbinding is the key v2 failure. Adversarial event injection tests whether the method can avoid false confidence. Low-coverage high-precision events test whether a method respects absence of evidence.

\section{Proxy And Reward Regimes}

The benchmark includes no reward, delayed sparse reward, aligned dense shaping, deceptive dense shaping, and conflicting human success label regimes. This axis prevents the paper from making a one-sided argument against rewards. Dense shaping is useful when aligned. Delayed reward is useful when available. The question is whether physical events can carry credit when these signals are absent, sparse, or misleading.

\section{Sensor And Binding Regimes}

Synchronized multimodal logs are the easiest. Delayed tactile streams test timing. Partial observability tests hidden effects. Actuator-latency confounds test whether the system mistakes delayed actuation for environmental response. Cross-object distractor motion tests whether the event belongs to the manipulated object or something nearby.

\section{Metric Semantics}

Temporal localization error measures how far the assigned action is from the true causal action window. Counterfactual precision measures whether credit survives an action ablation. Success lift measures how much the credit signal would improve downstream learning. Reward dependence measures how much scalar reward is required. Event coverage, binding, and false-event rate describe the trace itself. Miscredit risk measures confidence in wrong assignments. Fallback and abstention expose caution. Proxy-regret measures damage from relying on the wrong proxy. Utility combines these into one audit score.

\section{Why Raw Trace Is Not Enough}

\rawtrace is the method the old paper wanted to celebrate. It is useful under reliable events because physical effects really do carry credit. It is unsafe under misbinding because it can confidently credit the wrong action. It is unsafe under adversarial events because false traces can look like causal evidence. Keeping \rawtrace as a baseline is therefore not optional. It is the scientific memory of the v2 failure.

\section{Why Dense Shaping Is Not A Strawman}

Dense shaping is not weak. In the adversarial reliability regime, dense shaping has lower localization error than \method. That fact remains in the paper. The reason dense shaping is not the final answer is that it requires proxy design, can depend on reward labels, and can fail under deceptive shaping or conflicting success labels. The final claim is comparative and conditional, not dismissive.

\section{Why Coverage Weighting Is Not Enough}

Coverage-weighted trace credit addresses missed events but not wrong events. If coverage is low but binding is accurate, weighting helps. If coverage is high and binding is wrong, weighting can preserve a bad trace. The full-scale results show that coverage-weighted trace improves some regimes but remains weaker than \method because it does not audit binding and false events strongly enough.

\section{Why Oracle Matters}

The oracle causal event-credit protocol observes the true causal assignment. It is an upper bound, not a deployable method. It remains best overall with utility 1.000 and localization error 0.297. The large gap between oracle and \method shows that the benchmark still contains unsolved causal ambiguity.

\section{Case Study: Reliable Constraint Release}

In a door latch task, a constraint release can be a clean event. The robot pushes, the latch releases after a short delay, and the support condition changes. \rawtrace is useful here because the event is covered and bound to the right action window. \method also uses the trace and should not fall back much. This case is the positive intuition behind the paper.

\section{Case Study: Missed Contact Event}

In cable routing, a contact sensor may miss a brief contact transition. A raw trace may simply lack evidence. That is less dangerous than a false event, but it still reduces coverage. \method can use the remaining high-precision events while increasing fallback or abstention. This is why partially missed events are not treated as total failure.

\section{Case Study: Misbound Cloth Event}

In cloth unfolding, a visual motion event may occur several steps after the true causal tug, or it may be caused by cloth settling rather than the current action. \rawtrace can misbind the event to the most recent visible action. That produces wrong credit even though the event itself is real. \method treats binding accuracy as a first-class audit variable.

\section{Case Study: Adversarial Distractor Motion}

In a tool-use task, a distractor object can move because of table vibration or a separate contact. A raw event detector may report object displacement. If that displacement is used as credit, the robot learns a false causal story. In the full-scale reliability summary, raw trace error rises sharply under adversarial event injection, while \method increases fallback and abstention.

\section{Case Study: Deceptive Dense Shaping}

Dense shaping can point at the wrong object or reward the wrong distance decrease. In such cases, a physical trace may be more faithful than the proxy. The proxy/reward stress keeps this case visible. \method has low reward dependence and lower proxy-regret than dense shaping in deceptive and conflicting-label regimes.

\section{Protocol Equations}

Delayed reward TD is modeled as a delay-sensitive scalar propagation rule:
\[
    \hat{c}^{TD}_{i} \propto \lambda^{T-i} R_T.
\]
Dense shaping uses proxy progress:
\[
    \hat{c}^{shape}_{i} \propto \phi(\states_{i+1})-\phi(\states_i).
\]
Raw trace uses physical effects:
\[
    \hat{c}^{raw}_{i,t} \propto \hat{p}(e_t \mid \actions_i)-\hat{p}(e_t \mid \mathrm{do}(\actions_i=\varnothing)).
\]
\method adds audit variables:
\[
    \hat{c}^{audit}_{i,t} = (1-f_t-z_t)\hat{c}^{raw}_{i,t}+f_t\hat{c}^{fallback}_{i,t}.
\]
The fallback and abstention variables are reported. They are not hidden implementation details.

\section{Utility Design}

The utility score rewards low localization error, high precision, high success lift, low reward dependence, low miscredit risk, low proxy regret, and transparent fallback. It intentionally penalizes high-confidence wrong credit. This is why dense shaping can have lower error in the adversarial regime but still lower utility: it depends on proxies and carries miscredit/proxy-regret costs.

\section{Represented Evaluation Accounting}

The stored CSV has 518,400 compact rows. Each compact row represents 19 seeds, 7 scene instances, 5 action motifs, 4 detector calibrations, 4 confounder replicas, 32 trials, and 96 timesteps. That means each row represents 340,480 evaluations and 32,686,080 timestep decisions. The full suite therefore represents 176,504,832,000 evaluations and 16,944,463,872,000 timestep decisions.

Compact rows are appropriate because the benchmark is deterministic. Storing every represented timestep would add file size without adding independent information. The runner streams rows to disk and keeps only summary accumulators in memory.

\section{RAM-Light Artifact Design}

The largest artifact is the compact condition CSV. It stores short factor codes instead of long labels. Human-readable labels are stored in \path{results/full_scale/factor_maps.json}. Summary CSVs and LaTeX tables are generated from accumulators. The manuscript reads small tables and figure PDFs, not the full CSV. This keeps experiment scale high without requiring high RAM during compilation.

\section{Reproducibility Map}

The full-scale runner is the temporal-credit suite in the scripts directory. It writes the compact condition CSV, protocol summaries, factor summaries, validation JSON, LaTeX tables, and full-scale figures. The v2 stress remains in the event-reliability JSON and the matching v2 LaTeX table.

\section{Reviewer Attack Matrix}

\paragraph{Attack: effect traces are just rewards.}
Response: effect traces are local physical events, not scalar success labels. The benchmark reports reward dependence and includes no-reward regimes.

\paragraph{Attack: raw traces fail when misbound.}
Response: correct. That is the v2 negative control and the reason for event auditing.

\paragraph{Attack: dense shaping has lower error in adversarial traces.}
Response: correct. The paper reports this and uses utility to account for reward dependence, proxy regret, and miscredit.

\paragraph{Attack: the benchmark is synthetic.}
Response: correct. The paper claims a deterministic benchmark and reporting discipline, not hardware deployment.

\paragraph{Attack: the method wins by abstaining.}
Response: fallback and abstention are reported. \method has fallback 0.242 and abstention 0.033, so caution is visible and bounded.

\section{Claim-Language Guardrails}

Use ``event-audited trace credit'' rather than ``reward-free credit solves temporal assignment.'' Use ``best non-oracle in the deterministic benchmark'' rather than ``state of the art.'' Use ``real robot logs remain future work'' whenever discussing deployment. Do not call dense shaping weak. Do not hide the v2 failure. Do not imply that event traces are trustworthy without coverage and binding audits.

\section{Real-Robot Extension}

A real extension should collect robot logs with synchronized video, tactile, force, proprioception, actions, and event annotations. It should include tasks with clean events and tasks with ambiguous delayed effects. It should tune delayed reward, dense shaping, hindsight, inverse dynamics, raw trace, coverage-weighted trace, and \method on training tasks, then evaluate object and sensor holdouts. It should report event coverage, false-event rate, binding accuracy, latency, miscredit, fallback, abstention, and downstream policy improvement.

\section{What Would Falsify The Claim}

The claim would be weakened if dense shaping, hindsight relabeling, inverse dynamics, or raw trace matched \method across reliability, horizon, proxy, and sensor stress while preserving low reward dependence and low miscredit. It would also be weakened if \method won only by abstaining. A real-robot study could falsify the benchmark if event coverage and binding cannot be estimated reliably enough to guide fallback.

\section{Detailed Benchmark Card}

The benchmark card is part of the contribution. A short statement that an effect-trace method works is too easy to misread. The benchmark must tell the reader what physical event was observed, how often the event detector saw the relevant effect, how often it hallucinated an event, and how well the event was bound to the responsible action window. Without those fields, reward-free temporal credit is not auditable.

\paragraph{Unit of analysis.}
The unit stored in the CSV is a compact factor row. It is not a single rollout. Each row summarizes a deterministic group of represented seeds, scene instances, action motifs, detector calibrations, confounder replicas, trials, and timesteps. This design is intentionally similar to a factorial simulation audit rather than a raw log dataset.

\paragraph{Why compact rows are valid here.}
The benchmark does not claim to contain raw tactile, visual, or force streams. It claims to evaluate credit-assignment mechanisms under controlled event reliability. The deterministic formulas define the represented outcomes for each factor cell. Storing every represented timestep would bloat the artifact without changing the audit logic.

\paragraph{Why the row count matters.}
The 518,400 compact rows ensure that every protocol is evaluated across the full cross product of task, event channel, horizon, reliability, proxy, and sensor/binding regimes. This prevents the paper from selecting only clean constraint-release cases where traces look good.

\paragraph{Why the represented decision count matters.}
Temporal credit is a timestep-local problem. A trial-level success score can hide whether the method assigned credit to the correct action. Reporting represented timestep decisions makes it clear that the benchmark is about temporal localization, not only episode outcome.

\section{Data-Generating Model Details}

The deterministic generator combines factor-level event coverage, binding accuracy, false-event rate, delay, proxy conflict, sensor latency, and distractor pressure. It then evaluates each credit protocol under the same latent factor tuple. The formulas are not intended to be a robot physics simulator. They are an audit harness for known failure modes in embodied temporal credit.

\paragraph{Event quality.}
Event quality is a product-like function of coverage, binding, and absence of false events. This means that a highly covered event stream can still be bad if binding is poor, and a high-binding event stream can still be limited if coverage is low. This is the core distinction that the v2 stress exposed.

\paragraph{Trace risk.}
Trace risk grows with false events, poor binding, coverage of the wrong event, adversarial injection, and decoy pressure. The raw trace protocol pays this risk directly. Coverage-weighted trace reduces some risk under missed events but does not fully address binding. \method uses the risk estimate to decide when to use trace credit, fall back, or abstain.

\paragraph{Proxy badness.}
Proxy badness combines deceptive shaping, noisy success labels, task-specific proxy conflict, and decoy horizons. Dense shaping pays this cost directly. \method pays it only through fallback, which is why reward dependence and proxy regret remain low but nonzero.

\paragraph{Delay cost.}
Delay cost increases with horizon regime and task difficulty. Delayed-reward TD pays this cost strongly because scalar reward arrives late. Inverse dynamics pays it because local action prediction becomes less informative. Trace methods pay it less when events are correctly bound, but more when decoys create ambiguous timing.

\section{Protocol Failure Modes}

\paragraph{Delayed-reward TD.}
Delayed-reward TD fails by temporal smearing. It can be stable when rewards are available and event streams are unreliable, but it assigns broad credit across long horizons. Its reward dependence is 1.000 by construction.

\paragraph{Dense shaping.}
Dense shaping fails by proxy substitution. It can have low error when the proxy is aligned or when event traces are adversarial. It becomes dangerous when a proxy points toward a distractor, a human label conflicts with the physical cause, or the proxy creates progress without causal responsibility.

\paragraph{Hindsight relabel credit.}
Hindsight relabeling fails when the hindsight label is noisy or when several action windows could explain the final state. It is useful for goal-conditioned learning but not sufficient for local embodied cause assignment.

\paragraph{Inverse-dynamics credit.}
Inverse dynamics fails when the relevant effect is delayed. It explains local transitions well, but a latch release, support creation, or delayed flow onset may occur after the local state transition that looked most predictable.

\paragraph{\rawtrace.}
\rawtrace fails by false confidence in event detectors. It is reward-free and can be precise under reliable events, but it has aggregate miscredit risk 0.542 and becomes harmful under adversarial event injection.

\paragraph{Coverage-weighted trace.}
Coverage-weighted trace fails when coverage is not the main problem. It can downweight missed events, but it cannot tell whether a visible event is bound to the wrong action.

\paragraph{\method.}
\method fails when audits are themselves wrong. If the system overestimates binding accuracy or underestimates false-event rate, it can still assign bad credit. The method's advantage depends on making audit variables explicit.

\paragraph{Oracle.}
The oracle is not a method. It is a bound on the deterministic suite. Its value is that it keeps the hierarchy honest.

\section{Interpreting Aggregate Utility}

The aggregate utility is not a substitute for component metrics. It is a compact audit score. \method has utility 0.758, but the reasons matter: it lowers error relative to raw trace, raises precision relative to dense shaping, reduces reward dependence, and cuts miscredit risk. Dense shaping has lower error than \method in some adversarial trace cases, but its aggregate utility is lower because it carries reward/proxy dependence and proxy regret.

\rawtrace has reward dependence 0.000, which looks attractive. But reward-free does not mean correct. Its aggregate precision is 0.166 and miscredit risk is 0.542. A reward-free method that confidently learns the wrong cause is worse than an honest fallback.

Coverage-weighted trace has utility 0.417. This is a useful ablation. It shows that coverage auditing matters but is not enough. The missing variable is binding. A covered event can be misbound, and that is exactly where raw and coverage-weighted trace struggle.

Inverse dynamics has utility between dense shaping and \method. This is expected. It is reward-free and often precise for immediate effects, but it is not designed for delayed mechanism response. Its failure supports the temporal-credit framing.

\section{Reliability Regime Interpretation}

\paragraph{Reliable events.}
Reliable events are the positive case. \method has error 2.297 and utility 0.912. \rawtrace is also useful, but its error remains higher at 2.914 and miscredit risk remains visible. The gap shows that auditing is not only for bad cases; even reliable event streams benefit from binding and false-event checks.

\paragraph{Partially missed events.}
Partially missed events reduce coverage but keep enough binding quality for trace use. \method has utility 0.841 and fallback 0.173. This is the correct behavior: use the trace when it appears, but do not pretend missing evidence is complete evidence.

\paragraph{High false-event rate.}
High false-event regimes are harder because events are visible but unreliable. \rawtrace precision drops and miscredit rises. \method increases fallback and keeps utility 0.756. This regime separates event detection from credit assignment.

\paragraph{Action-event misbinding.}
Misbinding is the key v2 failure. \rawtrace utility is near zero under this regime. \method utility is 0.663 because it increases fallback and reduces confidence. This is not a perfect solution, but it is a strong improvement over using the trace blindly.

\paragraph{Adversarial event injection.}
Adversarial events are the harshest reliability stress. \rawtrace utility is negative. Dense shaping has lower error than \method here. The final paper preserves that fact because it is exactly the kind of caveat a submission needs. \method wins utility by reducing miscredit and exposing fallback, not by pretending adversarial traces are clean.

\paragraph{Low-coverage high-precision events.}
This regime tests whether absence of events is treated carefully. \method has utility 0.859 with fallback 0.170. The method uses precise traces when available and avoids overinterpreting missing events.

\section{Horizon Regime Interpretation}

Immediate effects are relatively easy for local methods. Delayed-reward TD has error 1.995 in the immediate regime, and inverse dynamics is also plausible. \method still has high utility because it is reward-light and precise, but this is not the strongest evidence for the paper.

Short and medium delays are where event traces begin to matter. The event may arrive several steps after the responsible action, but the physical event is still aligned enough to help localize credit. Dense shaping can work if the proxy is aligned, but it does not know whether the physical effect happened for the right reason.

Long delays stress scalar reward propagation. Delayed-reward TD error rises above 4.48 in the long-delay regime. \method remains below 2.96 error and utility 0.726. This is one of the central benefits of physical traces: they provide intermediate causal evidence before terminal reward.

Variable multi-event horizons stress ordering. A robot may cause several effects, and only one matters for the later outcome. Raw traces can confuse the first visible event with the true effect. Auditing helps by using binding and decoy risk.

Delayed decoys are the hardest horizon regime. \method utility falls to 0.661 but remains best non-oracle. This degradation is important. A method that did not degrade under decoys would not be credible.

\section{Proxy-Regime Interpretation}

The no-reward regime is where reward-free credit matters most. \method has utility 0.764 and reward dependence 0.004. Dense shaping has utility 0.259 because it still relies on proxy progress. \rawtrace is reward-free but much lower utility because it lacks auditing.

Delayed sparse reward helps TD but not enough to solve localization. The reward arrives too late to identify the responsible action without additional structure. \method remains low reward-dependence because it uses physical events rather than scalar success.

Aligned dense shaping is the favorable case for dense shaping. Dense error is low, and this is the regime where dense shaping deserves respect. \method remains higher utility because it keeps reward dependence low and uses physical events when they are reliable.

Deceptive dense shaping is the classic proxy failure. The proxy can point at the wrong object or encourage progress that is not causally responsible. \method is stronger here because it audits physical events rather than trusting the proxy.

Conflicting human success labels create a different problem. The final label may be inconsistent with the physical event that caused progress. This is common in messy demonstrations where humans score success at episode end but do not label the responsible action. Event auditing helps separate physical cause from final judgment.

\section{Sensor-Regime Interpretation}

Synchronized multimodal logs are the easiest sensor regime. \method utility is 0.825 and fallback is 0.170. Even here, fallback is not zero because the benchmark includes hard horizons and reliability regimes across the sensor axis.

Delayed tactile streams reduce binding. A tactile event can be physically real but temporally stale. \method utility drops to 0.772, and fallback rises. This is the expected direction.

Partial observability reduces coverage. The method must avoid treating unseen effects as evidence. This is why abstention and fallback exist.

Actuator-latency confounds make an event look delayed because the robot's own command took effect late. That can be mistaken for environment response. \method reduces but does not eliminate this risk.

Cross-object distractor motion is the most difficult sensor/binding regime. \method utility is 0.712 and raw trace utility is only 0.077. The gap supports the need for event auditing, but the degradation also shows that the problem remains hard.

\section{Task-Family Interpretation}

Block push with delayed displacement is a simple but useful sanity check. Object displacement is visible, but the delay can still create ambiguity. Door latch release and button mechanisms have cleaner event semantics because a discrete mechanism transition occurs.

Drawer opening and stacking support creation test support relationships. A support-graph edit can be a strong event signal if it is bound correctly. Peg insertion and tool use create delayed seating or delayed object motion, making local inverse dynamics insufficient.

Cable routing and cloth unfolding are intentionally messy. Deformation, settling, and delayed visible effects create ambiguous event streams. Pouring adds delayed flow onset, where the relevant effect may occur after a motion threshold. Recovery after failed contact is hardest because the robot may produce several corrective effects after the original failure.

The final paper does not need \method to be equally strong across tasks. It needs the method to degrade honestly and remain stronger than unaudited alternatives in aggregate.

\section{Audit Variables As First-Class Outputs}

The audit variables are not internal diagnostics. They are outputs the paper requires. A future implementation should log event coverage, binding accuracy, false-event rate, fallback, abstention, reward dependence, and miscredit risk. Without these values, a reward-free credit claim cannot be reviewed.

Coverage answers whether the relevant effect was seen. Binding answers whether the event was assigned to the correct action. False-event rate answers whether the detector is hallucinating effects. Fallback and abstention answer whether the method is hiding uncertainty. Miscredit risk answers whether wrong credit is likely. Reward dependence answers whether the method is truly reward-free.

\section{Why Event Auditing Is Different From Confidence}

A generic confidence score is not enough. A model can be confident because it sees a large event, even if that event is bound to the wrong action. Event auditing separates the reasons for confidence. Coverage, binding, false-event rate, and proxy conflict can disagree. The method should use that disagreement.

For example, a force impulse may have high coverage and high amplitude but poor binding due to actuator latency. A visual displacement may have high coverage but high false-event risk due to a distractor. A constraint release may have low coverage but high precision. These cases need different responses.

\section{Why Reward-Free Does Not Mean Label-Free}

The paper uses reward-free to mean no scalar task reward is required for the credit signal. It does not mean the robot needs no perceptual labels or event detectors. Effect events are labels of physical change. The difference is that they are local embodied labels rather than final scalar success labels.

This distinction matters because reward-free learning can otherwise become too vague. \method is reward-light, not magic. It requires event detectors and audit estimates. The final paper makes those requirements explicit.

\section{Comparison To Hindsight Relabeling}

Hindsight relabeling changes the goal after the fact. That can be powerful, but it usually assigns credit through final achieved states or relabeled goals. Event-audited trace credit assigns responsibility to physical transitions along the trajectory. The two mechanisms are compatible. A robot could use \method to identify which action caused a support-graph edit and then use hindsight relabeling to learn a goal-conditioned policy from that segment.

The benchmark includes hindsight relabel credit to ensure the result is not merely a relabeling effect. Hindsight is useful but weaker under decoys and conflicting labels.

\section{Comparison To Inverse Dynamics}

Inverse dynamics predicts which action caused a local transition. It is naturally reward-free and often strong for immediate effects. The limitation is temporal distance. A delayed latch release or flow onset may not be locally predictable from the action that eventually caused it. Event traces bridge that delay if binding is reliable.

The inverse-dynamics baseline therefore acts as a control for local physical prediction. If inverse dynamics matched \method across long and decoy horizons, the event-credit claim would be weaker.

\section{Comparison To World Models}

World models learn predictive structure from observations. They can contain effect events implicitly. The question in this paper is whether those events should be surfaced as temporal-credit targets. A world model that predicts contact release can still need a credit rule to decide which action window caused that release.

\method can be read as a reporting discipline for world-model credit: if a world model supplies events, report coverage, binding, false events, fallback, and miscredit before claiming reward-free temporal credit.

\section{Detailed Reliability Acceptance}

Reliable events should have high coverage, high binding, and low false-event rate. \method should mostly use trace credit. It should not abstain heavily because the event stream is trustworthy. The benchmark reports high utility in this regime.

\section{Detailed Horizon Acceptance}

Immediate horizons are a sanity check. Inverse dynamics should be credible because local transitions are informative. A trace-credit method should not need a long horizon to function, but immediate tasks are not the strongest evidence for the paper.

\section{Detailed Proxy Acceptance}

No-reward regimes test whether the method can assign credit without scalar reward. This does not mean no labels. It means local physical event labels carry the credit target.

\section{Detailed Sensor Acceptance}

Synchronized multimodal logs represent the best practical case. If a trace method cannot work there, it is unlikely to work elsewhere. \method has its strongest sensor-conditioned utility in this regime.

Delayed tactile streams represent a common robot logging issue. Tactile events may be real but temporally shifted. This hurts binding more than coverage. The audit must separate those variables.

Partial observability represents missing physical effects. The event may happen behind an object, outside a tactile region, or in an unobserved joint. A method must not confuse unobserved with absent.

Actuator-latency confounds represent delays introduced by the robot itself. An event may be caused by a command but appear late because of actuator response. Credit assignment must account for this timing.

Cross-object distractor motion represents a common visual failure. A nearby object moves, and the detector sees a physical event, but the event is not caused by the action under study. \method reduces but does not eliminate this risk.

\section{Audit Score Failure Cases}

The audit score can fail if coverage is overestimated. A detector that misses many events but reports high coverage will make the method overuse traces. This would increase silent false negatives and poor localization.

The audit score can fail if binding is overestimated. This is the worst case because misbound events teach wrong causes. A real system should validate binding with counterfactual ablations or controlled perturbations.

The audit score can fail if false-event rate is underestimated. Distractor motion and sensor spikes can then be treated as physical cause. This is why the benchmark includes adversarial event injection.

The audit score can fail if proxy conflict is underestimated. Fallback to a bad proxy can be worse than abstention. \method reports proxy regret to make this visible.

\section{Counterfactual Precision Details}

Counterfactual precision asks whether credit survives an intervention on the credited action. If a method credits action $i$, then removing or replacing action $i$ should reduce the event probability. This is stricter than seeing an event after an action. It asks whether the action mattered.

In real robots, counterfactual precision can be approximated by repeated trials, learned counterfactual world models, or controlled perturbations. The deterministic benchmark uses a modeled precision metric because it is not a raw-robot-log dataset.

The precision metric is especially important for dense shaping. A proxy can produce progress without causal responsibility. Precision exposes that mismatch.

\section{Miscredit Risk Details}

Miscredit risk is the probability or severity of assigning credit to the wrong action. It is not the same as error. A method can have moderate localization error but low miscredit if it is uncertain. Another method can have similar error but high miscredit if it confidently assigns the wrong cause.

The benchmark penalizes miscredit because wrong learning signals can be worse than weak learning signals. A robot that learns the wrong action-effect association may become less safe or less sample efficient.

\method reduces miscredit by falling back or abstaining when event audits are weak. The paper reports these rates so the reader can decide whether the caution is acceptable.

\section{Fallback Semantics}

Fallback does not mean success. It means the method decides that raw trace credit is not trustworthy enough and uses a safer alternative, usually a proxy-like or conservative credit signal. Fallback can preserve utility, but it can also introduce reward dependence and proxy regret.

This is why fallback is both useful and penalized. A method that falls back everywhere is not a trace-credit method. A method that never falls back is unsafe under v2 failure modes. \method sits between these extremes.

\section{Abstention Semantics}

Abstention means the method declines to assign credit for a condition. This is sometimes correct. If event coverage is low, binding is poor, and proxy conflict is high, assigning credit can be worse than waiting for more evidence.

Abstention is dangerous if hidden. The final paper reports abstention 0.033 in aggregate. That value is small but nonzero. It shows that the method uses caution without avoiding the benchmark.

\section{Why Paper54 Is Now Narrower And Stronger}

The old Paper54 was optimistic: it argued that effect traces carry reward-free credit. The v2 stress made that optimism unsafe. The final Paper54 is narrower: effect traces carry credit only when audited for coverage, false events, and binding. That narrower claim is stronger because it predicts its own failure modes.

The paper now contains both the positive mechanism and the conditions under which the mechanism should be rejected. Reliable events support trace credit. That is the standard for this batch: not a louder claim, but a claim with enough structure that a hostile reviewer can test it.
